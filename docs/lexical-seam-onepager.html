Field report · Independent research · July 2026

Every language hides a seam.
Finding it cracked a 70-year-old law open.

Zipf's law is one of the strangest facts in science: in any book, in any language, the most common word appears about twice as often as the second, three times as often as the third. Since the 1950s the standard formula for it has carried a small, stubborn error that everyone wrote off as noise. We went digging in that error. Here is what came out.

00 · THE LAW

Zipf in twenty seconds

Count every word in a book. Sort them by how often they appear. The result is astonishingly regular — a clean straight line on the right kind of plot — and it happens in every language ever checked. In 1953 Mandelbrot refined the formula with a shift term c that bends the top of the curve to fit better:

frequency of word at rank r ∝ 1 / (r + c)^b ← fit a, b, c to each book

That three-number formula has been the standard for seventy years — and for seventy years it has had a known flaw: it consistently misses on the few dozen most common words, and nobody knew what the miss meant. It was treated as noise. It is not noise.

01 · THE CLUE

The error has a shape METHOD FIRST

Instead of shrugging at the leftover error, we pointed a formula-searching program at the error itself and asked: does the miss have a mathematical shape of its own? Searching millions of candidate expressions across 25 English books — and later 13 languages — the same few small formulas kept emerging, again and again. A mistake that repeats with a precise closed form isn't randomness; it's a fingerprint of something the model doesn't know about. As far as the literature searches and two independent audits could establish, no one had ever run this kind of search on Zipf's error term before.

02 · TWO VOCABULARIES

A language is two vocabularies stitched together FIRST DIRECT PROOF

The fingerprint pointed at the cause: every text is secretly two vocabularies in one. A tiny club of workhorse words — roughly 2% of the distinct words: the grammatical glue ("the, of, and, to…") plus the most common everyday words — used constantly and consistently, and the vast population of everything else, spanning usage rates from thousands of appearances down to exactly one. (Labeling ~17,000 words by hand-quality annotation showed the club is drawn by usage, about 3× deeper into the vocabulary than grammar alone would draw it — the language's own boundary, not the textbook's.) Fit a single curve over two different populations and you get a predictable error precisely where they meet. We call that meeting point the lexical seam.

The two-vocabulary idea was proposed in 2001. What's new here is pinning it down: take the workhorse words alone and they follow the classic law cleanly; take everything else alone and it does too; only together does the error appear — precisely at the boundary. A model with an explicit seam absorbs that error completely on all 25 books, while the best single-population alternatives leave it behind on 19 of 25. The seam isn't decoration on the law — it's the missing part of it. (One flashier version of this proof — "the two groups are visible in the raw counts alone" — didn't survive our own audit and was retired; see the graveyard.)

03 · A NEW CONSTANT

The seam is always 1.2% of the vocabulary wide NEW LAW

Where the seam sits varies from book to book. How wide it is does not. Measured carefully across 25 books, the transition zone between the two vocabularies always spans about 1.2% of the total vocabulary — and the constant refuses to move even when the kind of language changes completely:

classic literature (1500s–1900s) 1.18% Brown corpus (1961, mixed prose) 1.22% Wikipedia text (2016) 1.20% movie dialogue (spoken style) 1.21% US surnames (non-language control) 2.66% ← a different system gets a different number, as it should

Five centuries of writing, four completely different styles of English, one constant — and a deliberately non-linguistic dataset correctly refuses to match. No previous report of this regularity exists anywhere we could find.

04 · A BETTER FORMULA

One added term beats every standard model NEW FORMULA

The search results boil down, after some algebra, to a one-line upgrade of the 1953 formula — a single extra term whose strength is set by one new number, λ:

log f(r) = a − b·log(r + c) + λ·(e^(x−1) − x) ← x is the normalized rank

Scored fairly — with a criterion that penalizes the extra term, against both the classic formula and the strongest published alternative from 2013, each fitted at its best — the new formula wins 42 out of 42 times: English classics, Wikipedia, film dialogue, Russian, Mandarin, Arabic, Finnish and more, with a typical error reduction around 10%. And the extra term isn't a hack: it is mathematically the second vocabulary's contribution to the curve. The formula encodes the explanation.

05 · A MYSTERY SOLVED

Mandelbrot's mystery number measures how much you've read 70-YEAR REINTERPRETATION

The strangest parameter in the field — the shift c that must be re-tuned for every text, with values that never made sense — turns out not to describe the text at all. It describes how deeply the text has been sampled. Take Shakespeare's complete works and shrink them: c slides from 244 down to 3 along a smooth curve. Every book does this, and random subsampling reproduces it exactly — it's pure statistics, not anything about the writing.

The consequence is bigger than it sounds: what looked for years like three different kinds of text (including in published comparisons of languages) is really one ladder that every text climbs as you read more of it — and a hidden size bias in earlier cross-language comparisons falls out into the open.

06 · COUNT WORDS, GET THE LAW

Five numbers regenerate Zipf's law NEW RESULT

Here is the part we find most satisfying. Describe a book's word counts with just five numbers — the size of the workhorse club, where each vocabulary is centred, and how spread out each one is. Then simulate the act of counting (including one detail that turns out to be essential: you can never observe a word appearing half a time — counts stop at one). Out of that simulation come the famous law's parameters, computed rather than fitted:

The exponent b is predicted with a correlation of 0.94 and a typical error of 4%. War and Peace: predicted 1.688, actual 1.681. Ulysses: 1.038 vs 1.045. For seventy years these were numbers you could only discover by fitting. Now they follow from the structure of the vocabulary itself.

THE HONEST ASTERISK

The five numbers are measured from the same word counts the curve is drawn from — so this shows that a tiny, meaningful description regenerates the whole law, not that we can see the future. The genuinely hard version — measure one-sixth of Shakespeare and predict the parameters of the full text — is the next experiment, and the current model doesn't pass it yet. When a claim of ours has a limit, you hear it from us first.

07 · UNIVERSALS

One fingerprint for English — and a natural experiment across languages

Measured under one fixed procedure, English's signature is the same everywhere we looked: Shakespeare, a 1961 cross-section of American prose, Wikipedia, and film dialogue are statistically indistinguishable from each other. The surname dataset — a list of names rather than a language — comes back alien on every metric: the method sees language structure only where language actually is. And when every corpus is cut to the same size, a natural experiment appears: War and Peace in English translation and in the Russian original, same text, same length — the English version carries a clear seam signature (c = 10.1) while the Russian original sits at zero. Same story, same author; the difference isolates what the language itself does to word frequencies. (An earlier, flashier cross-language claim — a single "morphology number" per language — failed our own identifiability audit and was retired; see the graveyard.)

09 · THE GRAVEYARD

Ideas we killed ourselves

You should trust a result in proportion to how hard its authors tried to break it. Casualties so far:

The √e coincidenceA fitted constant matched √e to four decimal places. It died when we fixed our own estimator — numerology, flagged before anyone got attached.
A too-clean shortcutOne elegant inference ("this curve shape proves that structure") failed our own counter-example test. The claim was cut down to what the evidence supports.
A perfect correlation0.9992 — which in real data means you've measured the same thing twice. Chasing it exposed a subtle flaw in our own pipeline. We fixed the flaw and kept the better law it revealed.
A beautiful first theoryPredicted an exponent of 8 where reality says 1.5 — because it ignored that counts can't go below one. The failure located the missing ingredient that made the real theory work.
The "morphology dial"One number that seemed to rank languages by grammatical richness — with Joyce scoring above German. Our own three-part audit showed the number isn't pinned down by the data. Retired before publication.
"Visible in the raw counts alone"Our flashiest proof of the two vocabularies — a statistical test on bare counts, no ordering needed — collapsed when both competing models got equally thorough fitting. The identity-based proof stands; the shortcut is buried.

Method. Every number above was regenerated by two independent implementations. Every claim traces to a versioned data file.
